\section{Stability of the FSA-v2 SDE: a Lyapunov analysis}\label{sec:lyap}

The point of the controller built in Section~\ref{sec:control} is to
maximise the time-averaged autonomic amplitude
$\frac{1}{T}\int_0^T A(t)\,\dd t$. The stability analysis below
should therefore not be read as ``where do trajectories settle?'' in
the abstract -- it should be read as ``where would training take
the athlete if the schedule $\Phi$ were held constant, and how does
that depend on the chosen training load?''. The honest answer is
that the FSA-v2 model exhibits a \emph{Goldilocks} structure: there
is an interior optimal training load $\Phi_{\mathrm{opt}}$ at which
the autonomic amplitude is largest; below it the body is
under-stimulated and $A$ is small; above it the body is
over-trained, fatigue rises quadratically through the
$\mu_{FF}(F-F_{\mathrm{typ}})^2$ term, and the autonomic oscillation
collapses. The job of the SMC${}^2$ controller is to find a
\emph{schedule} that beats the best constant-load policy on
finite-horizon objectives.

This section makes those statements precise. We first locate the
oscillatory equilibrium of the deterministic skeleton as a function
of the constant control $\Phi_c$, identify the optimal load
$\Phi_{\mathrm{opt}}$ and the overtraining-collapse threshold
$\Phi_c^{\mathrm{crit}}$, and demonstrate the Goldilocks shape. We
then return to the full SDE and exhibit a Foster--Lyapunov drift
inequality, ensuring that the controlled stochastic process is
exponentially ergodic for any bounded $\Phi$ schedule. Detailed
Jacobian computations and the Foster--Lyapunov drift calculation
are deferred to Appendix~\ref{app:lyap}.

\subsection{The two branches of the deterministic skeleton}\label{sec:lyap-branches}

Set $\sigma_B = \sigma_F = \sigma_A = 0$ in
\eqref{eq:fsa-B}--\eqref{eq:fsa-A} and a constant control
$\Phi(t) \equiv \Phi_c$. The deterministic skeleton has two
qualitatively distinct equilibrium branches.

\paragraph{The sedentary branch ($A=0$).} An explicit equilibrium
\begin{equation}
  B^*_0(\Phi_c) = \min\!\Bigl(\frac{\kappa_B \tau_B \Phi_c}{1+\varepsilon_A A_{\mathrm{typ}}},\,1\Bigr),
  \quad
  F^*_0(\Phi_c) = \kappa_F \tau_F (1+\lambda_A A_{\mathrm{typ}})\,\Phi_c,
  \quad
  A^*_0 = 0,
  \label{eq:eq-zero}
\end{equation}
exists for every $\Phi_c \ge 0$. (The clipping in $B^*_0$ reflects
the natural bound $B \le 1$; in practice $B$ saturates at unity for
$\Phi_c \gtrsim 2$.) This is the deterministic ``sedentary'' or
``de-trained'' state -- an athlete with whatever long-run
fitness/fatigue level the chosen training load supports, but with no
autonomic oscillation at all.

\paragraph{The oscillatory branch ($A > 0$).} A second equilibrium
exists wherever the Stuart--Landau growth rate at the
sedentary branch is positive, $\mu(B^*_0,F^*_0) > 0$, and is given
by the implicit relations
\begin{equation}
  A^*_{\mathrm{osc}}(\Phi_c) = \sqrt{\mu(B^*,F^*)/\eta},
  \qquad
  (B^*,F^*) = (B,F)\text{-equilibrium given } A^*_{\mathrm{osc}}.
  \label{eq:eq-osc}
\end{equation}
This is the physiologically interesting branch: a non-trivial
autonomic oscillation, sustained against the cubic damping by the
positive growth rate that the slow Banister state generates.

A short Jacobian computation (Appendix~\ref{app:lyap-jacobian})
proves the following.

\begin{proposition}[Local stability of the sedentary branch]\label{prop:stab-zero}
The sedentary equilibrium \eqref{eq:eq-zero} is locally
asymptotically stable if and only if
$\mu(B^*_0(\Phi_c),F^*_0(\Phi_c)) < 0$.
\end{proposition}

The two regions of $\Phi_c$-space are separated by the bifurcation
locus
$\{\Phi_c \!:\! \mu(B^*_0(\Phi_c), F^*_0(\Phi_c)) = 0\}$. The
oscillatory branch exists, and is locally stable, exactly on the
side where $\mu > 0$, i.e.\ \emph{below} the bifurcation threshold.

Three features stand out.

\paragraph{Persistent activity at rest.} At $\Phi_c = 0$, the
deterministic equilibrium of the slow Banister states is
$B^*_0 = F^*_0 = 0$, and the Stuart--Landau growth rate evaluates
to
\begin{equation}
  \mu(0,0) \;=\; \mu_0 - \mu_{FF}\,F_{\mathrm{typ}}^2
   \;=\; 0.036 - 0.40 \cdot 0.04 \;=\; 0.020 \;>\; 0,
\end{equation}
so the sedentary branch is \emph{unstable even at full rest}. The
model captures, correctly, that an athlete sitting at zero training
load still has a non-trivial autonomic amplitude --
$A^*_{\mathrm{osc}}(0) \approx 0.32$ -- driven by intrinsic
physiology rather than by exogenous stimulus.

\paragraph{An interior optimum near $\Phi_c \approx 2$.} The growth
rate $\mu(\Phi_c)$ rises with training load through the increase in
fitness $B$ (which lifts $\mu$) until $B$ saturates at unity around
$\Phi_c \approx 2$, and then falls as fatigue $F$ accumulates and
the quadratic suppression $-\mu_{FF}(F-F_{\mathrm{typ}})^2$
dominates. The maximum of $\mu$, and hence of
$A^*_{\mathrm{osc}}$, sits at the saturation point
\begin{equation}
  \Phi_{\mathrm{opt}} \;\approx\; 2.0,
  \qquad
  \mu_{\max} \;\approx\; 0.21,
  \qquad
  A^*_{\mathrm{osc}}(\Phi_{\mathrm{opt}}) \;\approx\; 1.02.
\end{equation}
This is the best a constant-load policy can do.

\paragraph{Overtraining collapse at $\Phi_c \approx 3.71$.} Above
the bifurcation threshold $\Phi_c^{\mathrm{crit}}$ the quadratic
fatigue-suppression term wins outright, $\mu$ drops through zero,
and the oscillatory branch coalesces with the sedentary branch
(now stable). At the truth values of Table~\ref{tab:fsa-truth} the
threshold is
\begin{equation}
  \Phi_c^{\mathrm{crit}} \;\approx\; 3.71
  \quad \text{(TRIMP-units / day)}.
  \label{eq:Phi-crit}
\end{equation}
Loads above this drive the system back to $A=0$ -- the autonomic
collapse that the Banister fitness/fatigue tradition documents
empirically and that motivated the introduction of the cubic
$\mu_{FF}$ term in the first place.

\subsection{Implication for the controller}\label{sec:lyap-implication}

Section~\ref{sec:lyap-goldilocks} establishes a baseline against
which the SMC${}^2$ controller's performance can be judged.

\begin{itemize}
  \item The best \emph{constant} schedule keeps $\Phi(t) \equiv \Phi_{\mathrm{opt}} \approx 2$,
        delivering an asymptotic time-average
        $\frac{1}{T}\int_0^T A(t)\,\dd t \to A^*_{\mathrm{osc}}(\Phi_{\mathrm{opt}}) \approx 1.0$
        once the slow Banister state has equilibrated. The
        finite-horizon canonical baseline used in Stage~D
        (Section~\ref{sec:results}) is the suboptimal
        $\Phi \equiv 1.0$, which delivers
        $A^*_{\mathrm{osc}}(1) \approx 0.81$.
  \item Any \emph{schedule} that beats the constant-$\Phi_{\mathrm{opt}}$
        average must do so by exploiting the transient dynamics of
        $B$ and $F$ over a finite horizon: front-loading training
        early to build $B$, then tapering to let $F$ recover before
        the horizon ends, etc. The Stage~D SMC${}^2$ result
        reports exactly such a front-loaded periodisation, with
        mean $\Phi \approx 1.96$, essentially recovering
        $\Phi_{\mathrm{opt}}$ as the time-average.
\end{itemize}

The $+28\%$ gain reported in Section~\ref{sec:results} over the
$\Phi \equiv 1$ baseline therefore decomposes, roughly, into a
``constant-load'' component (the optimal constant load $\Phi \approx 2$
already beats $\Phi=1$) and a ``schedule-shape'' component (the
non-constant front-loaded profile beats the constant
$\Phi = \Phi_{\mathrm{opt}}$ on the finite horizon by exploiting
transients). The first component is what the analysis in this
section accounts for; the second is what the SMC${}^2$ machinery
adds.

\paragraph{The sedentary saddle.} The $A=0$ branch enters the story
only as the saddle that the controlled trajectory has to push away
from when starting from a de-trained initial condition such as the
Stage~E5 starting state $(B_0,F_0,A_0) = (0.05,0.30,0.10)$. Because
$\mu(B^*_0,F^*_0)(\Phi_c) > 0$ for $\Phi_c \in [0,\Phi_c^{\mathrm{crit}})$,
the sedentary branch is repelling for any non-collapse training
load, and even small perturbations send the system toward the
oscillatory branch. The deterministic ``time-to-leave'' the
sedentary saddle scales as $1/\mu(B^*_0,F^*_0)$ -- another reason
to favour higher $\Phi$ early in a horizon when $B$ is small and
$F$ has yet to build.

\subsection{Stochastic stability and exponential ergodicity}\label{sec:lyap-stoch}

We turn from the deterministic skeleton to the full SDE
\eqref{eq:fsa-B}--\eqref{eq:fsa-A} on the operating domain
$\Xcal = [0,1] \times [0,\infty) \times [0,\infty)$. The
state-dependent diffusions vanish at the boundary, so $\Xcal$ is
positively invariant. We seek a single quantitative statement that
captures the long-time behaviour of the controlled stochastic
process. The Foster--Lyapunov framework supplies the right object.

\begin{definition}[Foster--Lyapunov drift]\label{def:foster-lyap}
A function $V:\Xcal \to [0,\infty)$ is said to satisfy a
Foster--Lyapunov drift inequality with rate $\alpha > 0$ and
constant $C \ge 0$ if the infinitesimal generator $\Lcal$ of the
SDE satisfies
\begin{equation}
  \Lcal V(x) \;\le\; -\alpha\,V(x) + C
  \quad\text{for all } x \in \Xcal.
  \label{eq:foster-lyap}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

Inequality \eqref{eq:foster-lyap} is the standard hypothesis that
delivers exponential ergodicity in the sense of total-variation
convergence to a unique invariant distribution
(Khasminskii, 1980; Meyn and Tweedie, 1993).

\begin{theorem}[Stochastic stability of FSA-v2]\label{thm:lyap}
Under bounded controls $\Phi:\R_+ \to [0,\Phimax]$, the FSA-v2 SDE
\eqref{eq:fsa-B}--\eqref{eq:fsa-A} satisfies the Foster--Lyapunov
drift inequality \eqref{eq:foster-lyap} for the quadratic Lyapunov
function
\begin{equation}
  V(B,F,A) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl(a B^2 + b F^2 + c A^2\bigr),
  \qquad a,b,c > 0,
  \label{eq:V-quadratic}
\end{equation}
with rate
$\alpha = \min\!\bigl(2/\tau_B,\,1/[\tau_F(1+\lambda_A A_{\mathrm{typ}})]\bigr)$
and a finite constant $C$ depending on the truth parameters,
$\Phimax$, and the choice of $a,b,c$.
\end{theorem}

The proof is a routine but tedious computation of $\Lcal V$
component by component, bounding each term using the boundedness of
$B$ on $[0,1]$ together with two applications of Young's
inequality on the $F$ and $A$ components; we record it in
Appendix~\ref{app:lyap}.

\begin{theorem}[Khasminskii, 1980; Meyn--Tweedie, 1993]\label{thm:ergodic}
Let $X_t = (B_t,F_t,A_t)$ be the FSA-v2 process under a fixed
bounded control $\Phi$, satisfying the conditions of
Theorem~\ref{thm:lyap}. Then $X$ admits a unique invariant
probability distribution $\pi^\Phi$ on $\Xcal$, and there exist
constants $K_0 > 0$ and $\rho \in (0,\alpha)$ such that, for every
$x_0 \in \Xcal$,
\begin{equation}
  \bigl\| \mathrm{Law}(X_t \mid X_0 = x_0) - \pi^\Phi \bigr\|_{\mathrm{TV}}
   \;\le\; K_0\,(1 + V(x_0))\,e^{-\rho t}
  \quad \text{for all } t \ge 0,
  \label{eq:ergodic-bound}
\end{equation}
where $\|\cdot\|_{\mathrm{TV}}$ is the total-variation norm.
\end{theorem}

The geometric content of Theorem~\ref{thm:ergodic} is that the
controlled stochastic process forgets its initial condition
exponentially fast, on a timescale $\rho^{-1}$ comparable to the
longest dissipative scale of the model. The invariant distribution
$\pi^\Phi$ depends on $\Phi$: under a constant load
$\Phi \equiv \Phi_c$, $\pi^\Phi$ is concentrated near the
oscillatory branch $A^*_{\mathrm{osc}}(\Phi_c)$ when $\Phi_c < \Phi_c^{\mathrm{crit}}$
and near the sedentary branch when $\Phi_c > \Phi_c^{\mathrm{crit}}$.
The control cost $\E\!\int_0^T A\,\dd t$ of \eqref{eq:cost} is
therefore, for long $T$ at constant load, well approximated by
$T \cdot \E_{\pi^\Phi}[A]$, which by the Goldilocks profile of
Section~\ref{sec:lyap-goldilocks} is maximised near
$\Phi_c \approx \Phi_{\mathrm{opt}}$. Beating that maximum requires
non-constant scheduling, which is exactly what the controller of
Section~\ref{sec:control} is designed to provide.

\begin{remark}[Limitations of the bound]\label{rem:lyap-limits}
The proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:lyap} in Appendix~\ref{app:lyap} uses
the simplest possible quadratic Lyapunov function and crude global
bounds on the residual factors of the drift. The constant $C$ that
emerges is therefore loose: it scales with $\Phimax^2$ rather than
with the time-average of $\Phi^2$, and ignores the cancellations
that the real dynamics exhibit near the operating point. A
sharper analysis -- perhaps with a piecewise Lyapunov function that
switches between the regimes $\mu < 0$ (overtraining) and
$\mu > 0$ (Goldilocks) -- would deliver a tighter rate and a
smaller constant. We do not pursue this here.
\end{remark}
